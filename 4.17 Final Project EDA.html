Questions: Exploring the Data

What is your outcome variable(s)? How well does it measure the outcome you are interested in? How does it relate to your expectations?

Our primary outcome variable is time spent on paid work. However, we initially ran regressions with wage as the outcome variable and found statistically insignificant results.

We did several multiple linear regressions with wage as the outcome variable. We expected wages to have significant results, when explanatory variables such as gender, childcare, and an interaction term of \((gender \times childcare)\) were included. We expected there to be a larger wage difference from the \((gender \times childcare)\) interaction term. However, we noticed small and insignificant coefficients for this regression. We’d expect time spent on childcare to be greater for women than men.

Further, there is a trade-off between time spent at paid work versus time spent on childcare. So, to interrogate this idea of the time trade-off, we ran a subsequent regression with time spent on paid work as the outcome variable. This regression includes gender and childcare as explanatory variables, with the \((gender \times childcare)\) interaction term. Here, we found significant results and a clear visual relationship of more time spent on childcare meant less time spent on paid work. This upheld our expectations, as both women and men exhibited this relationship.

What are your key explanatory variables?

  • Gender
  • Time spent on childcare
  • Time spent on unpaid domestic work

Research Questions

Is there a gender difference in how individuals allocate their time? How does the trade-off between time spent at paid work versus time spent on childcare differ by gender? Ultimately, the question of interest is how do demographics and expected responsibilities affect time use.

Questions: Data Wrangling and Transformation

What data cleaning did you do?

  • Converted the time variables from minutes to hours for easy interpretation
  • Removed rows with NAs
  • Other than that, the diary entry data was well cleaned and coded by IPUMs! 🙂
  • We focus on 2010-2018 samples, since historical data prior to then is sporadic and requires time series methods.

How did you wrangle the data?

We applied the following filters for the linear regression:

  • Married with kids: HHTYPE == 1
  • Child under 18: UNDER18 1 through 10
  • Post-college education: EDUC == 5 or 6
  • Full-time workers: FULLTIME == 1 (Only comparing men and women who work full-time)

Filtering was slightly less strict for Figures 1 through 4 (exploratory visualizations).

Are you deciding to exclude any observations? If so, why?

Yes, but this was mostly the data wrangling step. We removed any missing values or NAs. For some visualizations (Figure 2) a few of the time use variables were omitted to better depict explanatory variables, rather than activities we’re less interested in: such as travel, media consumption, physical activity, etc.

Did you have to create any new variables from existing variables? If so, how and why?

Yes, when performing PCA on the time variables, we decided to combine a few variables to increase interpretability. ACT_INHOME and ACT_OUTHOME represented two types of leisure, so were combined into ACT_LEISURE. ACT_PCARE and ACT_PHYSICAL, which represented personal care and physical activity, were combined into ACT_SELFCARE since physical activity (exercise) is also a form of personal/self care.

We also converted several variables into factors, to be used as categorical variables in our visualizations and preliminary analysis. For instance, SEX (formerly coded as 1 and 2) was re-coded into a binary FEMALE categorical variable, 0 for men and 1 for female.

filtered_data <- data %>%

    HHTYPE == 1, # filter to married with kids

    AGEYNGST >= 0 & AGEYNGST <= 18, # youngest child is between age 0 and 18

Figure 2: Average Daily Time Use by Sex

This visualization breaks down the 24 hours in a day into the proportion of time spent on various activities, averaged across the years 2010-2018. One of the time activities is our outcome variable, time spent on paid work, while others include time spent performing childcare, personal care, unpaid domestic work, and leisure.

# How time is split by gender on an average day!

# data wrangling
df <- filtered_data %>%
  filter(SAMPLE >= 2010 ) %>%  
  mutate(HHTYPE = as.numeric(as.factor(HHTYPE))) %>%
  mutate(
    Leisure = ACT_INHOME + ACT_OUTHOME
  ) %>% 
  select(-"ACT_CIVIC", -"ACT_EDUCA", -"ACT_MEDIA", -"ACT_MISSING",-"ACT_INHOME", -"ACT_OUTHOME", -"ACT_TRAVEL", -"ACT_PHYSICAL" )
# pivot! to have all activities listed under activity_category
df_long <- df %>%
  pivot_longer(
    cols = starts_with("ACT_") | starts_with("Leisure"),
    names_to = "activity_category",
    values_to = "minutes"
  )  %>% 
  mutate(
    # Clean up the activity names (optional)
    activity_category = gsub("ACT_", "", activity_category),
    activity_category = case_when(
      activity_category == "CHCARE" ~ "Childcare",
      activity_category == "PCARE"  ~ "Personal Care",
      activity_category == "UNDOM"  ~ "Unpaid Domestic Work",
      activity_category == "WORK"   ~ "Paid Work",
      TRUE ~ activity_category
    ),
    # Convert SEX codes to labels
    SEX = as.numeric(as.factor(SEX)), 
    SEX = case_when(
      SEX == 1 ~ "Male",
      SEX == 2 ~ "Female"
    )
  )

# aggregate the activities!
df_sum <- df_long %>%
  group_by(SEX, activity_category) %>%
  summarize(minutes = mean(minutes, na.rm = TRUE))

Figure 3: Clustering in 2D

This visualization uses hierarchical clustering to find groupings in PCA space. We discovered that data points in clusters 2 and 3, which represent the top right of the PCA space, are 69.8% female. This tells us that females are more associated with having younger children, and spending more time on unpaid domestic work and personal care!

Interpreting Principal Components (PCS): See the output summary generated by the code, where the first four PCs are defined. Each PC is a vector that represents a combination of factors. The most influential factors that make up each PC are described as follows.

PCA 1: Time spent on childcare, age of the youngest child, and the amount of children under 5

PCA 2: Time spent at work, time spent on self care, and time spent doing unpaid domestic work

Figure 5: Exploring the Research Question

In this visualization, we begin to observe the time trade-off outlined in our research question and hypothesis - there appears to be an opportunity cost associated with time spent at paid work versus time spent on childcare. There’s not an apparent difference between gender, however there’s a substantial split when time spent on childcare is less than 1 hour a day. While this could be noise, maybe we could interrogate this further in a regression. Otherwise, the gender split is minimal! Both exhibit a clear inverse relationship between time spent on paid work relative to time spent on childcare.
